Robot description

  This robot has potentiometers in the back controlling PID values, with an LED and photoresistors at the front angled downwards to sense the track (light shield coming soon). Connecting the two are a motor shield and Arduino, which connect to motors to make the whole robot run.

What is the purpose of the motor driver/shield?

  The purpose of the motor driver/shield is to connect the Arduino to the motors and allow the code run by the Arduino to run the motors. This also stops the motors from directly having access to the Arduino and provides places to solder header pins, making the whole setup more secure.

What was the best PID values that you used? How well did it do on this track? Any limitations?

  The best values we found for this robot were about 40 S, 25 P, 10 I, and 5 D. This track went pretty well, but we were limited in that a high enough speed could not be balanced fast enough by P, I, or D.


What does PID stand for? What is the purpose of the P, the I, and the D? What steps did you take to choose the best values for your robot? Explain this like you would for someone taking this course next year.

  PID stands for proportional integrated derivative. P makes a change proportional to the error, I makes a change based on the integrated error (cumulative error), and D makes a change based on the derivative of error (difference in error between now and the last one). To find the best values, we just experimented a lot with the different knobs. For someone taking this course next year, I would explain that P makes the robot turn a little better and D makes the robot wiggle less, but I makes the robot move smoother overall (sometimes). Any of the three values in excess makes the robot wobble a lot, so you have to experiment to find what works best.

Challenge #7: Explain why you need to calibrate your photoresistor values. What problem does this help solve and what problems do you still see coming from your photoresistors?

  You need to calibrate the photoresistor values to figure out what readings are of a white surface and what readings are of a black surface. This allows the robot to actually find where the maximum reading is. Otherwise, the robot wouldn't have any reference and wouldn't know where to go. However, if the calibration is different than the actual area, then the robot still might get confused. Furthermore, changes in light might mess with the photoresistors, requiring a light shield.
